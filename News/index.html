Subject: Phishing Trends in 2026: AI Now Drives 86% of Attacks 
Hi team,

KnowBe4 just released its Volume 7 phishing trends report, and a couple of patterns are worth knowing about because they change the playbook attackers use against staff:

Roughly 86% of phishing campaigns over the past six months were generated or assisted by AI. That means the spelling, formatting, and tone in a fake message are now consistently as polished as a real one -- the old "look for typos" advice is no longer a reliable filter.

Internal team impersonation showed up in about 30% of Q1 attacks. Expect messages that appear to come from a coworker, your manager, or someone in accounting, often referencing a real project or vendor by name.

Attackers are also pivoting beyond email -- calendar invites with malicious links and Teams/SMS messages are increasing.

What to do:
- Treat any unexpected request to move money, change payment details, share credentials, or open a sensitive document as suspicious, even if it looks like it comes from someone you know.
- Confirm by phone or in person before acting on requests of that kind.
- If a calendar invite or message links to a sign-in page you weren't expecting, don't sign in -- navigate to the service directly in your browser instead.

When in doubt, forward it to me.

Thanks,
Larry
Subject: Watch for Spoofed Emails Redirecting Shipments 
Hi team,

The FBI issued a warning this week about a wave of cyber cargo theft. The pattern is straightforward: attackers compromise (or convincingly spoof) a freight broker or carrier email account and then send updated shipping or pickup instructions that quietly redirect a load.

For our jobsite logistics -- deliveries of materials, equipment moves, subcontractor shipments -- this is the kind of scam where the email looks completely legitimate because it sometimes is coming from a real, but compromised, vendor inbox.

What to do:
- If a broker, carrier, or supplier emails a change to a pickup location, drop-off address, payment details, or driver information, confirm it by phone using a number you already have on file (not the number in the email).
- Be especially cautious with last-minute changes, urgency framing ("driver is en route, need new address now"), or requests to re-route a shipment to an unfamiliar location.
- If something feels off, hold the shipment and call us before responding.

Our email filtering catches obvious fakes, but a compromised legitimate vendor inbox will look perfectly normal -- the verification call is what stops this.

Thanks,
Larry
